# Vision Methodology

Source: Ebi Atawodi (Director of PM at YouTube, ex-Netflix, ex-Uber) via Lenny's Podcast.

---

## What a Vision Is (and Is Not)

A **vision** is a vivid, specific picture of a future destination — not a mission, not a strategy, not a roadmap.

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Vision** | What the world looks like when you've arrived. Time-travel 3–5 years out and describe what you see. |
| **Mission** | The purpose and guiding principles for why you're doing it. |
| **Strategy** | The choices you make about which problems to solve in which order to reach the vision. |
| **Roadmap** | The delivery plan. |

> "A pilot who says 'Miami, 24 degrees, picture the beach' vs. one who says 'I don't know where we're going but I'm a good pilot.' The vision is the beach destination."

---

## Four Required Attributes of a Great Vision

Every vision must be all four of these:

1. **Lofty** — It almost scares you in an exciting way. Something you'd get up every morning for.
2. **Realistic and attainable** — Not so pie-in-the-sky that it feels out of reach. People need to feel "we could actually do this."
3. **Devoid of today's limitations** — Written in a vacuum from current tech constraints. The limitations of today may not be the limitations of tomorrow.
4. **Grounded in a clear, potent problem** — There must be a sharp, specific user problem at the core. Vague or manufactured problems produce hollow visions.

Check every vision draft against all four. If one is missing, revise before proceeding.

## Three-Stage Framework: Empathize → Create → Evangelize

### Stage 1: Empathize

Immerse deeply in customer problems before writing a single word of the vision.

**Research inputs to gather (apply to whatever context is available):**
- **Dog food**: Use your own product obsessively. Notice every friction point.
- **Cat food the competition**: Use competitors' products. Identify what you love about them and why.
- **Living problem doc**: A cross-functional list of the top 10 known problems with the product. Contributed by PM, UXR, data science, engineering, ops, and content strategy — each asked to stack-rank independently.
- **Stakeholder input template**: Force stakeholders to give you their top 10 problems (not 50 slides). Stack-ranked. This gives multifaceted input and earns buy-in.
- **Data + intuition**: Use data to update your mental model of the customer. But don't outsource judgment to research — conviction is the PM's job, not the data's.

**Strategy session structure (if you have the time):**
- Day 1: Insights — understand users, do app teardowns, get expert inputs
- Day 2: Strategy — pick which problems to focus on, in which order
- Day 3: Big Rocks — the 3–5 biggest things to ship

### Stage 2: Create

Synthesize your problem understanding into a picture of the future world.

**Core question to answer:** "If we solve this problem completely, what does the world look like?"

**Narrative techniques to apply (pick at least one):**

#### A. The "Once Upon a Time" Mad Libs Framework
Map the vision as a story:
```
Once upon a time, [the state of the world / the problem].
And then, [something happened that made it worse].
And because of that, [consequence for users].
And one day, [by YEAR, the change you will create].
And because of that, [the downstream effect on users / the market].
And finally, [the lasting dent you left in the world].
```
The "one day" line is the actual vision statement. Use it.

#### B. The TechCrunch / New York Times Headline
- Write the headline you want to see published when your vision comes true.
- Add the subheadline.
- Optionally: write the full article.
- Mock it into the actual TechCrunch or NYT template so it looks realistic.
- Test: "What is the impact? What feeling do I leave people with?"
- Example: "Uber wants to replace your Clipper card."

#### C. App Store Screenshot Exercise
- Print 4 blank rounded rectangles (simulating app store screenshots).
- Ask: "If we solved all these problems, what would these screenshots say?"
- Forces prioritization — you can only show 3–4 things. The big rocks surface naturally.
- Good for workshop settings with the team.

#### D. The Design Sketch / Pencil Drawing
- Work with a design partner to draw the future state — even just on Post-its.
- A sketch communicates more viscerally than words.
- Post-its are enough to start. The designer will polish it.
- The image should make people excited before the product exists.

### Stage 3: Evangelize

Share the vision in concentric circles, outward.

**Three concentric circles:**
1. **Core team** — Present at team meetings multiple times. Share the doc with comments *open* (not view-only). People need to steep in it. Re-present until it percolates.
2. **Stakeholders** — Teams that play a part in delivering the vision. They'll add perspectives. That's OK — the goal is that people are bought into the story.
3. **Leadership** — Go as big as possible. Let leadership pull you back. Don't self-censor the ambition. Let them amplify the story.

**Practical tips:**
- Give the narrative doc a short link (e.g., `go/team-vision`) so anyone can find it.
- Use the vision when onboarding new team members and candidates — the vision does recruiting work.
- Expect a lag — a new vision may take 18 months to enter the planning cycle. Be patient; keep repeating.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

| Pitfall | What to do instead |
|---|---|
| No vision at all | Every PM at every level needs one — even for a small feature area. |
| Confusing mission with vision | Mission = why/purpose. Vision = the destination picture. |
| Vision constrained by today's tech | Think beyond current limitations. Tomorrow's constraints may not be today's. |
| Unclear or weak problem at the core | Sharpen the problem first. A hollow problem produces a hollow vision. |
| Giving up at the evangelism stage | Expect naysayers. Re-present patiently. A vision may take 18 months to land. |
| Using research as a substitute for judgment | Research updates your mental model. Conviction is your job. |
| A 6–12 month vision | That's a newsletter. Real visions are 3–5 year horizons. |
| Rebuilding the vision from scratch each year | Vision is Evergreen. Add fidelity and specificity over time; don't restart. |
| Not picking a lane (peanut-buttering) | Conviction means picking what you think is right, even under uncertainty. |

## What "Vivid and Specific" Means

Avoid corporate-speak at all costs. The vision statement must:
- Name the user and describe the transformed experience in sensory terms
- State a time horizon ("by 2027", "within 3 years")
- Be a picture, not a platitude

**Bad:** "We will create a seamless, world-class upload experience."
**Good:** "Selling at {{COMPANY}} will feel as fast and effortless as uploading a story to Instagram — point your camera, and you're done."

**Test:** Can you close your eyes and see it? If yes, it's vivid enough.

---

## The Narrative Document Structure

The living team doc (Insights → Strategy → Big Rocks) is separate from the vision document but feeds it:

- **Insights**: Top 10 known problems, cross-functional contributions
- **Strategy**: Which problems to solve, in which order, and why
- **Big Rocks**: The 3–5 sequenced priorities (put rocks in first, then sand — not a laundry list of 20 things)

Keep this doc Evergreen. Version it (2024, 2025, etc.). Update quarterly or semi-annually.
