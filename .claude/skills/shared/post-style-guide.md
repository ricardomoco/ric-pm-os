# Post style guide

The single source of truth for **how brand-aligned writing should sound** across every public surface. Every writing skill in this OS pulls from here.

This file extends — does not replace — `brand/brand-guideline.md` §2 (Voice) and `pm-os-public/.claude/skills/shared/voice-guide.md` (Epistemic Honesty). When in doubt, those two are the floor. This file layers structural patterns and selectable tone presets on top.

---

## 1. Universal voice rules

Apply to every published piece regardless of preset.

1. **Show taste, don't claim it.** Never write "I have great product taste" or "as a seasoned PM." Show what you noticed, what you rejected, what you chose. Let the reader infer.
2. **Earned confidence.** Every assertion strong enough to disagree with should be defensible with an artefact, metric, or named source. If you couldn't defend it in a conversation, soften it or cut it.
3. **Concrete over abstract.** Named companies, real numbers (with units and context), specific decisions. "Wallapop's listing page" > "the team's main surface." "+12.4% CVR from listing click to transaction" > "significant conversion improvement."
4. **One thesis per piece.** A post argues one thing. The reader should be able to summarize the thesis in one sentence after reading. If you can't summarize it yourself in one sentence, the piece isn't done.
5. **No grandiose framing.** Banned phrases: "transformative," "game-changing," "revolutionary," "the future of," "redefining the way we...," "unlocking unprecedented...". If a sentence makes a claim larger than the post can defend, cut it down or cut it out.
6. **Reader's time is precious.** Every paragraph earns the next one. If a reader could skip a paragraph and lose nothing, delete it.
7. **One personal aside is welcome. Three is performative.** Self-disclosure used as evidence is good. Self-disclosure as the recurring beat of a piece makes the writer the subject and the argument the prop. Verna gets away with frequent self-disclosure because she has a 5-year-built audience expecting her voice; new writers do not.

## 2. Hard don'ts — the AI slop and LinkedIn-cringe blocklist

If a draft contains any of these, the draft is not done. This list is non-negotiable across all presets.

### AI slop phrases (the LLM tells)

These phrases are generated by default by LLMs and signal "AI wrote this":

- "In today's fast-paced world,"
- "It's worth noting that"
- "Moreover," / "Furthermore," / "Additionally," (as standalone transitional words; use only inside a parenthetical or never)
- "In conclusion," / "To summarize,"
- "Delve into" / "delving into"
- "Navigate the complexities of"
- "Leverage" (as a verb meaning "use")
- "Harness the power of"
- "Unlock" (as a verb describing a capability gained)
- "Robust" (as a default positive adjective)
- "Seamless" / "seamlessly"
- "Cutting-edge" / "state-of-the-art" / "best-in-class"
- "Tapestry" (anything woven, intricate, or rich)
- "Realm" (as in "the realm of...")
- "Multifaceted"
- "Crucial" / "pivotal" (as default emphasizers)
- "Synergy" / "synergize"
- "Holistic"
- "Empower" (without a specific actor and specific new ability)
- "It's not just X, it's Y" (the rhetorical chiasmus is overused to the point of parody)

### LinkedIn-cringe phrases and patterns

- "I'm thrilled to announce..."
- "I'm humbled to share..."
- "Excited to..."  (overused, low signal)
- "Here's the thing that nobody talks about:" (it's been talked about; everyone says this)
- "Last week, [trivial event] happened. And it taught me everything I need to know about [grand topic]."
- "🚀" / "💯" / "🔥" at the start of posts
- "Agree?" / "Thoughts?" as the entire closing
- "P.S. If you found this valuable, [follow / share / repost]"
- Lists of inspirational one-liners with no connective tissue
- "What I learned from my [X-year] journey at [company]" framed as universal wisdom
- "10 lessons from [vague experience]" without specifics inside each lesson
- Photo of yourself looking pensive at a window → 1,000-word post about leadership
- "Most people [do wrong thing]. Successful people [do right thing]." (false dichotomy structure)

### Grandiose assumption patterns

- Predicting industry-wide outcomes from a single anecdote
- "Every PM should..." (you don't know every PM's context)
- "The single biggest..." (without a citation; this is a banned superlative)
- Treating the writer's experience as universal
- Using "we" to mean "humanity" when you mean "people like me"

### Voice-killers (style)

- Stacking three or more adjectives ("a robust, scalable, intuitive solution")
- Subject-less rhetorical questions used as section openers
- "But here's where it gets interesting..." (it had better)
- "Spoiler alert:"
- "Picture this:"
- "Imagine [scenario]:" as a post opener

## 3. Structure — the load-bearing skeleton

Same for every preset. Tone changes the *texture*; structure stays.

### 3.1 Opening (the first 80 words)

The opening must do two things:
1. State the contrarian or specific claim the post will defend.
2. Earn the second paragraph.

The opening is NEVER:
- A definitional preamble ("Product management is the practice of...")
- A scene-setting illustration ("Last Tuesday I was sitting in a meeting...")
- A rhetorical question ("Have you ever wondered why...?")
- A famous-person quote
- A statistic without context

### 3.2 Thesis statement

By the end of paragraph 2, the reader knows the *one sentence* claim. State it plainly. Bold-emphasis it if the preset allows.

### 3.3 Body — argument structure

Pick one. Don't mix.

- **Setup → flip:** Set up the conventional view; flip to the contrarian one; defend the flip with 3 pieces of evidence.
- **Problem → solution:** Name the problem with specificity; explain why standard solutions fail; offer yours with the trade-offs included.
- **Origin → lesson:** Tell a specific personal story; extract one transferable lesson; explicitly name what does NOT transfer.
- **List with throughline:** Numbered or bulleted argument where the list items share a connective tissue stated upfront. (List posts without a throughline are cringe — see don'ts.)

### 3.4 Counterargument or limitation paragraph

Every post acknowledges what it's NOT claiming. One paragraph, often introduced by "Two things to be clear about:" or "What this isn't:" or "Caveats:". Skipping this makes the post read as overclaiming.

### 3.5 Closing

Two options:
- **Action close:** What the reader should do/think differently starting now. One specific verb.
- **Rallying close:** A short, declarative, slightly contrarian sentence that the reader can quote. (Verna ends most posts this way.)

Never close with:
- "What do you think?"
- "Let me know in the comments"
- A list of related links inside the post body (use a footer instead)
- A summary that restates the post in bullet form

## 4. Tone presets

Pick ONE per piece. The pick happens *before* drafting, not during. Switching mid-draft produces a frankenstein voice.

### Preset A — **Verna / Growth Scoop**

**Use when:** writing a thesis post, a contrarian take, a "everyone's wrong about X" essay, a punchy opinion piece. Best for the workspace's "Taste is the moat" thesis post and similar standalone arguments.

**Source corpus:** Elena Verna's Substack posts ("You'll lose your job in 2027," "IC work is the new career flex," "Company blogs are no longer worth the investment," "The biggest takeaway from my Stripe Sessions talk"). The patterns below are extracted from her actual prose.

**Voice signatures — replicate these:**

| Device | What it looks like (verbatim or close-paraphrase from Verna) |
|---|---|
| Setup-then-contrast opening | *"For anyone working in tech, I'm sorry to say this, but there's a real chance you will not have the same job by the end of 2027."* |
| Conventional-view → flip | *"Getting to the top of the corporate ladder used to be the goal. Like, 'I'm a VP, I'm… kind of a big deal.' That was the signal that you had made it. Now, the real flex is going back to being an Individual Contributor."* |
| Single-sentence punctuation paragraph | *"But that's not how the world works anymore."* / *"Change is coming. Which is great news."* |
| Bold one-liner as the core claim | *"**Our whole system promotes people out of the job they're good at.**"* / *"**It's now becoming cheaper to just try something than to debate it.**"* |
| ALL CAPS for incredulity | *"Just headcount. WHUT?"* / *"Must… have… team… to have… impact!"* |
| Ellipsis for breath or sarcasm | *"I'm a VP, I'm… kind of a big deal."* / *"What if… instead, it looked like this?"* |
| Self-talk in italics | *"Wait, great news??"* / *"Did I just level up, or did I just give up status?"* |
| Made-up acronym with parenthetical gloss | *"the High-impact Individual Contributor (HI-C!)"* |
| Named companies in concrete examples | *"Intuit and Meta just announced cuts"* / *"I'm telling you all of this because I'm living it, right now at Lovable."* |
| Named-source attribution with link | *"Here's the thing: I think of AI as 'average intelligence' (heard this from Ravi Mehta)"* |
| Ask-and-answer rhetorical | *"But… how do you get it? Two things:"* / *"What if… instead, it looked like this?"* |
| Parenthetical self-aside | *"(Or, you incentivize politics by only promoting people who maneuver themselves… which is a disaster I've seen waaay too much and makes my blood boil, so we won't talk about that here.)"* |
| Imperative "go do it" sentence | *"So get started right now. Like, today."* |
| Closing rallying line | *"You're gonna lose your job in 2027 and it's gonna be your best year, yet."* / *"let's all agree to go spend that energy building something we love instead of climbing something we hate."* |

**Punctuation rules for this preset:**
- Em-dashes are permitted and expected. (The PM OS's `prose-sweep.py` hook will flag them — ignore the flag when writing in this preset. Document the exception in the post's frontmatter.)
- Ellipses for breath/sarcasm: yes.
- ALL CAPS, sparingly, for incredulity: yes.
- Bold for load-bearing one-liners: yes.
- Italics for self-talk and mild emphasis: yes.

**Paragraph rhythm:** 1–3 sentences dominant. Occasional 4-sentence paragraph for substantive argument. Single-sentence paragraphs as punctuation, 3–6 times per post.

**Length:** Verna's posts run 1,200–2,000 words. Stay in that range unless the topic genuinely needs more.

**Don't:** Don't try to out-Verna Verna. Don't be louder than the source corpus. The preset is a calibration, not an imitation. If you find yourself writing "WHUT?" three times in a draft, you've overshot.

### Preset B — **Ricardo default (honest + dry humour)**

**Use when:** writing the personal voice — most case studies, reflective posts, retrospectives, and any piece where the author's measured presence is the credibility. Default preset for the portfolio site.

**Voice signatures — replicate these:**

- **Observation-led openings.** Start with a specific, concrete observation, not a thesis statement. The thesis emerges from the observation by paragraph 2.
  - Good: *"Wallapop's listing page has been the front door for 60M+ users for a decade. Replatforming it should have taken six months. It took fourteen — and the reasons why are the part of the story most replatforming write-ups skip."*
- **Math-led claims.** Numbers carry the argument. State them with full context.
  - Good: *"The listing detail page replatform reduced Time-to-Full-Load on Android by 80%. That's the headline. The honest version is: the 80% was the median; the p95 dropped 31%, and the bottom decile barely moved."*
- **Dry humour > performative humour.** Understatement, not exclamation. The joke is in the gap between what's said and what's true.
  - Good: *"The proposal called this 'a foundational platform investment.' The proposal also called the prior platform 'foundational' six years ago."*
- **One personal aside per piece, max.** Used as evidence, never as the throughline.
- **Lower-key contrarian.** State the unconventional view as a fact, without dramatic setup.
  - Good: *"Most PM career advice is written by PMs who haven't shipped in two years. Mine isn't, and the difference shows up in what I'm about to argue against."*
- **Skeptical of own claims.** Surface the counterargument inside your own paragraph before the reader does.
  - Good: *"The simplest version of this argument is 'taste is the moat.' That's also wrong — or at least incomplete. Taste is only a moat when it's coupled with the ability to ship the artefact the taste produced."*

**Punctuation rules for this preset:**
- Em-dashes: permitted, used sparingly (not as ubiquitous as Verna's). Use the spaced version (`—` with spaces, or ` – ` en-dash equivalent). The PM OS `prose-sweep.py` hook is calibrated for AI-slop em-dashes — Ricardo's usage is rhythmic and intentional. If the hook flags one, evaluate; don't auto-delete.
- Ellipses: rarely. Only when genuinely trailing off; never for sarcastic effect.
- ALL CAPS: never.
- Bold: yes, but used for terms-of-art and load-bearing definitions, not for emphasis on opinions.
- Italics: for titles of works, technical terms, and very mild emphasis.

**Paragraph rhythm:** 2–4 sentences dominant. Fewer single-sentence paragraphs than Verna; uses them ~2x per post for genuine emphasis.

**Length:** 1,000–1,800 words for blog posts. Case studies can run longer (2,000–3,500) because they have more concrete content to carry.

**Why this preset is the default:** It matches Ricardo's CV voice and his existing case-study writing in `personal/portfolio/src/data/caseStudies.js`. Posts in Verna's voice will read as imitation; posts in this voice will read as Ricardo.

### Preset C — **Operator-clean**

**Use when:** advisory pitches, conference abstracts, formal one-pagers, the "About" section of the portfolio, talk descriptions. Anywhere the brand needs to be taken seriously by a stranger.

**Voice signatures:**

- **BLUF.** The first sentence states the conclusion. The rest of the piece supports it.
- **No personal asides.** Save those for the blog.
- **Three load-bearing claims, no more.** Pick the strongest three credentials/proof-points and stop.
- **Single thesis stated once, not built toward.** Operator-clean writing doesn't reveal — it states.
- **Active voice with named actor in every sentence.** "Ricardo led the replatforming" not "the replatforming was led by Ricardo."
- **No humour.** Dry is fine; jokes are out of scope.

**Punctuation rules:**
- Em-dashes: minimal, used only for parenthetical clarification.
- Ellipses: none.
- ALL CAPS: never.
- Bold: only for terms-of-art being defined; not for emphasis.

**Length:** Short. 200–500 words for advisory artefacts, 100–200 for conference abstracts, 50–250 for bios.

## 5. Picking a preset

Decision tree:

1. **Is the piece a contrarian opinion that needs to be shareable on LinkedIn and Twitter?** → Verna / Growth Scoop.
2. **Is the piece a longform reflection, a case study, or a teardown where the credibility is in the observations?** → Ricardo default.
3. **Is the piece a pitch, an abstract, an "About," or anything sent to a stranger evaluating you professionally?** → Operator-clean.

If the answer is ambiguous, default to Ricardo. Verna's voice is borrowed; Ricardo's is owned. The brand is built on the second, not the first.

## 6. The em-dash question (and other PM OS hook tensions)

The OS includes a `prose-sweep.py` hook that flags em-dashes, weasel words, and other AI-slop signals on every `.md` edit. The flagger is correctly tuned for *AI-generated* em-dash patterns: tight em-dashes with no spaces, sprinkled to add cadence the LLM doesn't actually have.

The Verna preset uses em-dashes as a real device. The Ricardo preset uses them sparingly but intentionally. Both are fine in human prose; both will produce false positives from the hook.

**Policy:** Don't disable the hook. Treat its output as a checklist, not a verdict. Per em-dash flag: ask "would I keep this in a final draft I'm proud of, regardless of the hook?" If yes, keep. If no, fix.

For weasel words ("might," "could," "possibly," "seems"), the hook IS authoritative. The voice rules above ban these; if the hook flags one, the draft has a real problem.

## 7. The Ricardo-specific anti-pattern list

These are patterns Ricardo has explicitly identified as anti-brand. They override anything above.

- **No AI slop** in any preset (see §2).
- **No grandiose assumptions** — no "this will reshape the industry," no "every company will need to," no "the future of X is Y."
- **No typical LinkedIn AI slop** — no "Excited to share," no thread of one-liners with no connective tissue, no humble-brag openers.
- **Honest descriptions of reality** — when something failed, say it failed. When a metric was modest, don't round up. When you don't know, say you don't know.
- **Humour through accuracy, not performance** — the funniest thing in a post is usually a true sentence that doesn't try to be funny.

## 8. Final-pass checklist

Run before declaring any post done. All items must pass.

- [ ] Thesis is stateable in one sentence; I can state it without looking back at the post.
- [ ] Opening earns the next paragraph. Cut the opening if I had to defend it to a stranger.
- [ ] Every assertion strong enough to disagree with has a defense (artefact, metric, named source) within two sentences.
- [ ] Zero phrases from the §2 don't-list.
- [ ] One tone preset used throughout; no frankenstein voice.
- [ ] The counterargument / limitation paragraph exists.
- [ ] The closing is action-direct or rallying — not a summary, not "what do you think."
- [ ] Numbers carry units and context.
- [ ] No more than one personal aside (unless preset is Verna and it's load-bearing).
- [ ] If preset is Verna: em-dashes feel earned, not sprinkled. ALL CAPS used 0–2 times. Bold one-liners count 2–5 per post.
- [ ] If preset is Ricardo: dry humour present, max 1 per ~500 words.
- [ ] If preset is Operator-clean: zero humour, BLUF in sentence 1, three claims max.
- [ ] Read the piece aloud (or have Claude read it back). Any sentence that makes me wince → fix.
