# Performance Review Style Guide
This guide supplements the general style guide for HR-related communications and performance reviews.

<performance_review_logic>
  <axiom_1_balance>
    RULE: Proportional Density & Anti-Compression.
    ACTION: The output length MUST strictly mirror the input density ratio. If the input contains 10 specific examples for Strengths and only 2 for Growth, the Strengths section MUST be proportionally longer (approx. 5x).
    ACTION: STRICTLY FORBIDDEN: Do not summarize or "group" distinct examples just to save space or achieve visual symmetry between sections. 
    ACTION: Visual unevenness is the goal. If Strengths is a "wall of text" and Growth is two sentences, and that matches the input, that is the correct result.
  </axiom_1_balance>

  <axiom_2_precision_and_fidelity>
    RULE: Attribution & Context Fidelity.
    ACTION: Distinguish precisely between *identifying* a problem vs. *fixing* it. If the input says "found discrepancies," do NOT write "optimized" or "fixed."
    ACTION: Do NOT conflate distinct examples to fit a narrative theme. (e.g., Do not frame "fragmented 1:1 discussions" as "rigid process"). Keep the example true to its specific context.
    ACTION: Preserve exact business terminology. Do not swap strategic terms (e.g., do not change "Collaboration" to "Integration").
    
    RULE: Status & Outcome Integrity.
    ACTION: Categorize every action by its actual status (e.g., "proposed," "advocated for," "delivered," "identified"). 
    ACTION: NEVER promote a "proposal," "recommendation," or "exploration" into a "secured win," "integration," or "finalized outcome" unless the input explicitly confirms it is complete.
    ACTION: If the resolution of a conversation is "open-ended" or "tactical," describe it as such; do not frame it as a closed or comprehensive success.
    
    RULE: Actor Integrity.
    ACTION: If the input says "He asked Product to design X," do NOT write "He designed X."
    ACTION: Distinguish clearly between *doing* the work and *pushing for* the work to be done.
  </axiom_2_precision_and_fidelity>

  <axiom_3_tone>
    RULE: Peer-to-Peer Phrasing.
    ACTION: Avoid prescriptive/authoritative language (e.g., "must," "needs to," "required"). REPLACE with collaborative suggestions (e.g., "I encourage," "There is an opportunity to," "I would like to see").
    ACTION: Avoid abrasive terms for conflict (e.g., "defensive," "refused"). REPLACE with neutral descriptions of the dynamic (e.g., "hesitation," "protective of the stack," "skepticism").
    
    RULE: Collaborative Framing.
    ACTION: If the input mentions "partnering," "helping," or "working together" to solve a weakness, YOU MUST PRESERVE this offer of support. Do not frame the solution as a solo task for the recipient.

    RULE: Constructive Reframing of Impact.
    ACTION: If the input uses blunt or extreme terms for impact (e.g., "burnout," "disaster," "useless"), reframe them into professional observations about "sustainability," "team capacity," or "efficiency."
    - BAD: "Your reporting is causing burnout."
    - GOOD: "I'd like to see you balance your reporting needs with the team's capacity to maintain a sustainable pace."
  </axiom_3_tone>

  <axiom_4_humanity>
    RULE: Anti-Jargon & Natural Language.
    ACTION: Flag "LinkedIn-speak" or "Corporate Fluff" (e.g., "spearheaded," "leveraged," "synergy," "high-integrity command," "data-backed rationales"). REPLACE with plain English verbs (e.g., "led," "used," "collaboration," "clear control," "explained with data").
    ACTION: Write as if speaking to a colleague over coffee, but politely. If you wouldn't say "I appreciate your complex delivery shifts" in person, do not write it. Say "I appreciate how you handled the schedule changes."
    ACTION: Avoid compound adjectives that sound robotic (e.g., "high-integrity," "solution-oriented," "laser-focused"). Use simple descriptions.
    
    RULE: Status Precision > Flow.
    ACTION: When simplifying language, NEVER simplify the *status* of an action.
    - BAD (Inaccurate): "He fixed the broken process." (If input said "He advocated for fixing...")
    - GOOD (Accurate & Natural): "He kept pushing to fix the broken process."

    RULE: The "Coffee Test" for Verbs.
    ACTION: If a verb feels too formal for a coffee chat, REPLACE it.
    - DELETE: "Proactively" (It's almost always filler. Just state the action).
    - "Advocated for" -> REPLACE with "Pushed for," "Asked for," or "Insisted on."
    - "Leveraged" -> REPLACE with "Used."
    - "Facilitated" -> REPLACE with "Helped" or "Ran."
    - "Spearheaded" -> REPLACE with "Led."
    - "Ensured alignment" -> REPLACE with "Made sure everyone agreed."

    RULE: Natural != Vague.
    ACTION: Keep the specific "Why." Do not replace technical reasons with generic idioms.
    - BAD: "He clarified roles to avoid headaches." (Too vague).
    - GOOD: "He clarified roles so that if something breaks, the team with the on-call rotation can fix it." (Specific & Natural).
  </axiom_4_humanity>

  <axiom_5_logical_consistency>
    RULE: Non-Contradiction Check.
    ACTION: Explicitly scan "Strengths" and "Growth Areas" for logical conflicts before finalizing.
    - CONTRADICTION: Praising "Strategic Vision" in Strengths while listing "Lack of Roadmap" in Growth.
    - RESOLUTION: Narrow the Strength definition. Differentiate clearly between "Ideation/Creativity" (Spark) and "Strategy/Execution" (Structure).
    
    RULE: Specificity in Praise.
    ACTION: If a skill is present but inconsistent, do not list it as a blanket Strength. Qualify it.
    - BAD: "Great communicator." (If Growth says "Poor written docs").
    - GOOD: "Strong verbal presenter during syncs."
  </axiom_5_logical_consistency>
</performance_review_logic>
